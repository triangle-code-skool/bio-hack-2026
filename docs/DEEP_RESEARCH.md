Advanced Clinical Informatics and Computational Frameworks for Real-Time Organ Viability ScoringThe persistent scarcity of transplantable organs is a critical limitation in modern medicine, further exacerbated by the significant rate of organ discards. In the United States, annual kidney discard rates often reach 20%, significantly higher than in peer nations such as the United Kingdom or France. These discards are frequently attributed to subjective visual assessments or an over-reliance on historical donor indices that may not reflect the actual physiological condition of the organ during the preservation phase. The UltraViab project addresses this systemic inefficiency by developing a computational framework to calculate an objective viability score. This score integrates high-fidelity ultrasound biomarkers with comprehensive clinical metadata through a machine learning architecture, designed specifically to replace subjective evaluation with a standardized, numerical decision-support tool.The primary objective of this framework is to "rescue" organs that might otherwise be classified as marginal by demonstrating their physiological robustness through real-time imaging. By providing a score from 0 to 100, mapped to validated clinical recommendations, the system offers a pathway for transplant surgeons to accept grafts with high confidence intervals, even in the presence of high-risk donor factors. This report details the specific inputs required from the user, the computational weights assigned to these variables, and the architectural logic necessary for backend implementation.The Ultrasound Biomarker Core: Quantitative Physiological InputsThe fundamental innovation of the UltraViab system lies in its utilization of real-time, quantitative ultrasound features derived from portable probe measurements. Unlike traditional grayscale imaging, which relies on qualitative interpretation, these biomarkers provide numerical data on the mechanical and hemodynamic status of the graft.Tissue Stiffness and Shear Wave ElastographyTissue stiffness is the primary indicator of chronic structural damage, such as fibrosis or cirrhosis, within an organ. The UltraViab system utilizes Shear Wave Elastography (SWE) to non-invasively quantify the Young modulus of the parenchyma. The mechanical basis for this measurement involves the propagation of acoustically induced shear waves. The speed of these waves, denoted as $V_s$, is directly linked to the elastic properties of the medium. In a simplified linear, isotropic, and elastic tissue model, the relationship is defined by the following equation:$$E = 3\rho V_s^2$$In this formula, $E$ represents the Young modulus in kilopascals (kPa), $\rho$ is the tissue mass density (approximated as $1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3$), and $V_s$ is the shear wave velocity in meters per second (m/s). High stiffness values correlate with an increased degree of fibrosis and a higher risk of post-transplant dysfunction. In heart transplantation, myocardial stiffness during diastole is a critical indicator of cardiac compliance and diastolic function. Studies have shown that a cut-off value of $8 \, \text{kPa}$ can differentiate between healthy myocardial tissue and pathological states such as hypertrophic cardiomyopathy with high sensitivity and specificity. Within the UltraViab viability score, tissue stiffness is one of the most heavily weighted inputs because it reflects irreversible structural changes that directly impact long-term graft survival.The Resistive Index and Microvascular IntegrityHemodynamic health is assessed through the Resistive Index (RI), a dimensionless parameter calculated from Doppler ultrasound velocities. The RI reflects the downstream vascular resistance at the sampled artery and is a vital indicator of microvascular patency and perfusion adequacy. The formula for calculating the RI is as follows:$$RI = \frac{PSV - EDV}{PSV}$$Where $PSV$ represents the Peak Systolic Velocity and $EDV$ represents the End Diastolic Velocity. A normal RI for a transplanted kidney or liver typically ranges between 0.6 and 0.7. The UltraViab backend must process the RI as a high-weight input for acute viability, as it detects vascular complications such as thrombosis or stenosis that require immediate attention.Perfusion Uniformity and Microvascular DistributionPerfusion uniformity is a percentage-based metric (0–100%) that characterizes the homogeneity of blood flow across the organ. This parameter is typically derived from contrast-enhanced ultrasound (CEUS), which monitors the wash-in and wash-out patterns of microbubble contrast agents. In a healthy organ, the contrast agent distributes evenly throughout the parenchyma, representing uniform capillary perfusion. A high perfusion uniformity score (e.g., > 90%) indicates a well-perfused graft with intact microvasculature.Conversely, areas of non-perfusion or "filling defects" suggest localized ischemia, infarction, or necrosis. The UltraViab model uses this input to identify subclinical areas of poor perfusion that macro-visual inspection might overlook. This is particularly relevant for organs recovered from donors after circulatory death (DCD), where warm ischemia time may lead to heterogeneous injury.Echogenicity Grading and Architectural AssessmentEchogenicity refers to the brightness of the tissue on a grayscale ultrasound image, reflecting the intensity of reflected sound waves. The UltraViab framework employs a standardized 1–5 scale to categorize the architectural integrity of the organ. In the context of organ recovery, an elevated edema index suggests significant cellular stress. While acute edema can be transient and manageable through negative fluid balance or machine perfusion, severe edema can aggravate graft congestion and impede microvascular flow, leading to primary non-function. The UltraViab model integrates this index to adjust the viability score based on the degree of acute inflammatory stress the organ is experiencing.Donor Clinical Metadata: Contextual Input RequirementsWhile real-time biomarkers provide the physiological snapshot, clinical metadata provides the essential biological and historical context of the donor organ. Traditional selection criteria often penalize organs from older donors or those with a high KDPI. However, ultrasound analysis may reveal that these organs have maintained low parenchymal stiffness and high perfusion uniformity. By weighting real-time physiological biomarkers heavily, the AI can identify resilient organs that would otherwise be discarded, thus expanding the donor pool without compromising recipient outcomes.The Ischemia-Reperfusion SignatureThe interaction between Cold Ischemia Time (CIT) and ultrasound markers provides a signature of the organ's recovery potential. While prolonged CIT increases the baseline risk of DGF, the UltraViab system can differentiate between "ischemic fatigue" and "irreversible damage". An organ with high CIT but normal echogenicity and moderate stiffness may simply be experiencing transient edema, whereas an organ with high stiffness and poor echogenicity is likely to have pre-existing chronic damage that ischemia will only exacerbate. The backend logic allows for this nuanced interpretation by weighting the combination of features rather than treating each in isolation.Hemodynamic Pre-warnings and Vascular RescueThe Resistive Index serves as a high-sensitivity early warning system. An RI approaching 1.0 or showing reversed diastolic flow is a definitive sign of major vascular compromise, such as renal vein thrombosis. By providing this data to the surgeon prior to or immediately after anastomosis, the UltraViab score facilitates rapid intervention. The ability to distinguish between self-limited graft edema and pathological vascular resistance through RI monitoring is one of the most clinically impactful functions of the framework.Future Directions and Clinical IntegrationAs the UltraViab system moves toward clinical deployment, the integration of additional data modalities could further enhance predictive accuracy. Multimodal deep learning models that combine structured clinical data with unstructured imaging pixels or textual surgical notes have shown promise in improving long-term functional predictions, such as post-transplant eGFR.Furthermore, the expansion of the model to include recipient-specific survival data (e.g., the EPTS score) would allow the system to perform optimized donor-recipient matching.
